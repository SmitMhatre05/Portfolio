LiveTrack

Timeline: March 2025 - April 2025

Role: UX & Product Designer

Overview

Crowded gyms are a recurring issue we face, and often hear others mention in conversations and on social media. Some gym apps provide the overall occupancy data for the users to view, however, it doesnâ€™t show how people are distributed throughout the gym. This approach leads to unanticipated congestion and a disrupted workout experience.

The LiveTrack feature addresses this problem by using a real-time heatmap over the gym floor plan to show how busy the gym and each specific section is based on its capacity and occupancy, which can be used to view specific areas to better plan workouts and avoid congestion. In addition, gym managers can also use these insights to identify patterns and make informed decisions to improve the gym experience for everyone.

User Research

Going into user research, my main target group was individuals aged 17-35 years old. This group includes university students, young professionals, and early-career individuals who visit the gym a few times per week. Many lead busy lives and value efficiency, aiming to get the most out of their workouts in limited time.

To better understand their needs, I designed a user survey featuring a mix of multiple choice and short answer questions. After analyzing the responses, four key pain points clearly emerged.

Competitor Analysis

01. Anytime Fitness

The Anytime Fitness app shows how busy each location is and includes predictive data for different times throughout the week. Users can also use the app to access the gym.

02. PureGym

The PureGym app offers similar features to the Anytime Fitness app. It has a live occupancy counter and also includes predictive data for different times throughout the day.

03. Google

Google provides a bar graph of epected crowd levels based on past trends and a rough estimate of how many people are currently present in the gym.





The Problem -> A common limitation across all of these solutions is the lack of detailed visibilty into specific gym zones, which leads to the pain points uncovered in my user research and impacts the gym experience.

The Solution

How This Works

Designing the heatmap solution was a fun and creative challenge, but what also interested me was figuring out how to make it actually work in the real world, and that curiosity led me down a deep rabbit hole of research.

For the live occupancy tracker, the system updates in real time whenever someone signs in with their key fob or their phone. Depending on the gymâ€™s policies, users might also be able to use their phones to check in. The counter adjusts again when they sign out.

When it came to the real time heat map, I focused on finding a solution thatâ€™s affordable, realistic, and respects user privacy. The best option I found was mmWave radar sensors. These use high-frequency radio waves to detect how many people are in a space and where they are, without any cameras. They work by sending out signals and measuring how they bounce back, and are usually mounted on ceilings or high walls to cover large areas. Because they donâ€™t collect images or personal data, they comply with privacy standards and is one of the key reasons I chose this method.

Design Inspiration

The idea of adding a heatmap to the gym floor plan came from traffic maps that use color to show how crowded areas are. I wanted to apply that same concept to the gym, but in a way that feels more personal and useful to everyday users. Instead of just being a tool for data analysis, the heatmap gives users a clear, visual way to understand how busy the gym is and decide the best time to go.
