PROBLEM OVERVIEW

The ER is Inefficient

As a part of our GBDA 301 term project, I worked in a team of six to explore challenges faced by ER nurses. In high-pressure environments, nurses must care for patients while dealing with the consequences of fragmented systems; such as scattered information, delayed updates, and added stress. These frequent breakdowns increase the risk of errors and slow down critical care.

Patients feel it too...

The inefficiencies of fragmented systems affect patients as much as staff. During several hospital visits this year, I experienced ER wait times of nearly three hours, even at a hospital recognized for its hospitality. These challenges are not only limited to well-resourced hospitals. They are often more severe in facilities without advanced management systems or adequate staffing. This prompted us to ask...

How might we help ER nurses monitor patients, prioritize tasks, and respond to condition changes in real time to reduce delays and risks in care during high-pressure situations in the ER?

THE SOLUTION

CENTRAL - Centralizing Information for Nurses in the ER

Central is a wrist-worn display product that centralizes patient information, tasks, and alerts into one place, allowing nurses to act quickly without being slowed down by fragmented systems and helping them stay focused on patient care instead.

Logging In

Nurses would use their fingerprint to log in securely and quickly.

Main Dashboard (Emergency State)

This screen displays a patient map in the hospital and a task list, with emergencies color coded as red, allowing nurses to prioritize and respond quickly.

Patient Profile Details

Upon clicking on a patient from the patient map, the nurse can see a general overview of the patient's information and status.

Log Your Task

Once the nurse has treated a patient, they can log the completed task for record-keeping and communication with other staff. This is not an official medical record.

Main Dashboard

Once the patient emergency has been dealt with, they are brought back to the main dashboard screen, where they can continue to monitor other patients and manage tasks.

Logging Out

Once the nurse is done their shift, they can log out so the next nurse can log in securely.

DISCOVERY RESEARCH

A Deeper Look at the Current ER Landscape

I led the discovery research process, curating nine research papers to find three core issues linked to our problem:
  • The communication gap between patients and doctors & patients and nurses
  • Systematic & operational issues in the ER and their impact
  • The fragmentation of information throughout the ER

To synthesize my research findings, I developed two journey maps and a service blueprint that illustrate the workflows and user experience both before and after our proposed solution.

PROTOTYPING

Bringing it to Life

Alongside co-designing the user interface, I created a low fidelity prototype of the actual product. Although the prototype was simple and low‑budget, it effectively conveyed our concept, which was our main goal.

TESTING

Validating Our Solution

We tested our product through a bodystorming session, simulating an ER waiting area with a check-in desk and two patient rooms. The note-takers at the front desk took note of the think-aloud feedback and pros/cons from each run. The scenario included a nurse, two patients, and a narrator to guide the flow. Using tables and chairs, we set up a simple floor plan to mirror the ER environment. From this testing, we identified three key findings:

01

Increased Efficiency

Users found the patient-location and task-tracking features helpful. They made it easier for ER nurses to stay organized and focus on key tasks, improving overall efficiency and directly supporting our research question.

02

Improved Response Times

The users liked the real-time updates, especially for noticing changes in patient conditions. Their feedback showed how important this feature is and confirmed that our product supports quick, informed decisions.

03

Clean UI

Users felt the UI presented information clearly without becoming overwhelming, which is especially important in an ER setting where the environment can quickly become crowded and overstimulating.

EXPERT FEEDBACK

Intuitive and Relevant

During our presentation, we shared our work with healthcare professionals and researchers from the University of Waterloo’s Systems Design Engineering faculty. Their feedback highlighted both the strengths of our solution and areas for further consideration. Our final product + pitch received a 94% score.

01

Recognition of the Problem

Experts stated that fragmented information is a major issue in healthcare today. They appreciated that our solution tackled this challenge directly.

02

Accessibility Concerns

They were curious about how the product would support nurses with limited arm mobility and how the interface could be adjusted to accommodate color blindness.

03

Implementation Challenges

They raised concerns about logistical and financial barriers and said the ambitious concept would need careful planning before it could be used in practice.

Key Takeaways

Refinement is Key

The biggest lesson I learned is that refinement matters. We began with a digital hologram watch and narrowed it to a simpler, more practical arm sleeve for the ER. Focusing the scope helped us decide what to keep and what to cut, ensuring the solution addressed the core problem instead of trying to do everything at once. With more planning, we plan on tackling the logistical challenges that are difficult to anticipate in a classroom setting.
